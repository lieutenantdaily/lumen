If this increase passes, The City of Athens will be spending $11,220 per student per year – more than 95% of school districts in the state. The City of Athens doesn’t have a revenue problem – it has a spending problem.



30-year Tax

The proponents of this tax say that it will be a “temporary tax.” The facts: this tax, by law, will be in place for at least 30 years. To most of the people affected by the tax, that is not temporary – it is a lifetime. This tax hits established small business owners and seniors very hard – for most of these people, they will never see the end of this tax. Plus, when have you ever seen a “temporary tax?” The odds of this tax ending when it is currently set are virtually zero, according to historical trends.

Again and Again

We are being told that this tax increase is “for the schools.” It is to improve on our current buildings and help build new ones. Isn’t that what we were told the alcohol tax was for? And the sales tax? When does it end? It is time that government, on every level, learns to live within its means and stop asking us for more and more of our hard-earned money. What’s to say that they won’t be back in a few years asking for more?

The current City Property Tax is 10 mills. This tax increase would raise it to 22 mills – a 120% increase! Even if you combine the City, County, and State Property Taxes, the rate still goes from 40 mills to 52 mills – a 30% increase.

Misplaced Priorities

The Athens City School Board had millions to spend on a new administrative building for themselves, but claims they need a tax increase to put safe rooms in schools. Don't be a victim of their money mismanagement. Vote no on August 25th!

Lack of Transparency

Where is this money going? We are being told that a certain amount of the money will go to repairing current schools, building new ones, and putting storm shelters in existing schools. But how do they expect to be held accountable for these claims? Unlike traditional tax increase proposals, these taxes are not earmarked in any way. It all goes straight into the budget.

A responsible and accountable School Board and City Council would have put each individual item on the ballot as a separate tax increase – repairs, new schools, and storm shelters. They would have let the voters choose which increase they wanted, and ensured that the money would go where they said it will go. But instead, we have a total lack of transparency on this issue. Vote NO to hold the City Council accountable for their spending habits and ensure fiscal responsibility.

Dear Citizens,

This proposed tax will severely impact all property owners in the city, especially small business owners and renters, who will likely see their rates rise as a result of this massive tax increase. A 12-mill increase in one vote is not only extraordinarily high, but completely unprecedented – no county or municipality anywhere in Alabama has even attempted such a large increase in recent years. We will be raising awareness of the upcoming vote and encouraging Athens citizens to vote no.

According to City-data.com, the median home value in Athens is $150,000. That means that YOU, as a homeowner, will pay an additional $180 per year in taxes. That’s $5,400 over the life of the tax!

According to recently published data from the State School Board, Athens already spends over $10,300 per pupil per year. That means Athens already spends more than 90% of other school districts in the state…and that’s before factoring in this additional tax increase they want!

I encourage you to join our coalition of homeowners, small business owners, renters, and other Athens citizens in sending a message to the school board and city council that enough is enough. We already passed an alcohol tax and a sales tax in recent years. Athens doesn’t have a revenue problem; it has a spending problem!

If you would like to join us with financial support, please donate.

Sincerely,


John Wahl
